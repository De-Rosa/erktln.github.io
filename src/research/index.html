Applications of Computing in Scientific Research

Introduction

"The most emerging tool in the research process is the computer" (1). This statement is echoed by the widespread use of computer algorithms in academia. These can range from pure number-crunching to complex analysis. There are many examples of computing in the sciences. For example, phycisist might need to know how to create a simulation, while a mathematician might need to compute large equations. Many undergraduate science degrees even require some module in the basics of designing computer algorithms for this purpose.

Data Processing

Computers can process large amounts of data with incredible speed compared to the average person.
This creates improvements in productivity and can open opportunities in analysing data that might have seemed impossible before. Sometimes, this can even lead to disproving theorems (Euler's sum of powers conjecture).

Literature Review

Computers can store records of journals and other scientific documents which can be used for a more optimal search of past literature or, alternatively, compare a finalised document to both detect and prevent plagiarism.

Data Storage

Computers can also be used to store large databases in a more compact and accessible form than a non-virtual counterpart like books. The World Wide Web enables sharing this data accross institutions and country borders, simplifying collaboration

Communication

Computer-based communication is not restricted to data. Here are some examples:

  • Social media can be used to share research with the world, especially less scientific communities.
  • Video calling brings the possibility of live collaboration, regardless of location. This means both an increase in remote jobs and international cooperation.
  • Even ignoring these, simple messaging software has numerous implications for productivity.
